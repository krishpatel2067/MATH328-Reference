\section{Fourier Series}

\subsection{Introduction}

Given a $2L$-periodic function $f(x)$, its Fourier series is given by

\begin{equation*}
    f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left[ a_n \cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) + b_n \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \right]
\end{equation*}

where

\begin{align*}
    a_n & = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^L f(x)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)~dx         \\
    b_n & = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^L f(x)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)~dx\text{.}
\end{align*}

Note that the $a_n$ formula applies for $a_0$ too with $\cos(0) = 1$. The constant $a_0/2$ is also the average value of $f(x)$.

\vsp

The Fourier series shows that a $f(x)$ can be represented as a sum of even (cosine) and odd (sine) functions. Thus, the following is true:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $f(x)$ is even $\implies$ $b_n = 0$
    \item $f(x)$ is odd $\implies$ $a_n = 0$
\end{itemize}

\vsp

The constants $a_0/2$, $a_n$, and $b_n$ are the components of $f(x)$ in the directions of the basis functions

\begin{equation*}
    \left\{ 1, \cos\left(\frac{\pi x}{L}\right), \sin\left(\frac{\pi x}{L}\right), \cos\left(\frac{2\pi x}{L}\right), \sin\left(\frac{2\pi x}{L}\right), \dots \right\}\text{.}
\end{equation*}

The convergence of the Fourier series relies heavily on the coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$ since the trigonometric functions simply oscillate between $-1$ and $1$.

\subsection{Complex Form}

The Fourier series can be equivalently written in complex form as

\begin{equation*}
    f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty c_n e^{i\frac{n\pi x}{L}}
\end{equation*}

where

\begin{equation*}
    c_n = \frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^L f(x)e^{-i\frac{n\pi x}{L}}~dx\text{.}
\end{equation*}

The $i$ and $-i$ in $f(x)$ and $c_n$ can be swapped without changing the result.

\vsp

Note that $c_n = c_{-n}^*$ iff $f(x)$ is real-valued. Similar to the non-complex form, each $c_n$ is the component of $f(x)$ in the direction of the basis function $e^{i\frac{n\pi x}{L}}$.

\subsection{Parseval's Relation}

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^L |f(x)|^2~dx = \frac{a_0^2}{4} + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{n=1}^\infty (a_n^2 + b_n^2) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty |c_n|^2
\end{equation*}

where $|z|^2$ for a complex value or function $z$ is $z z^*$, so

\begin{equation}
    |c_n|^2 = c_n c_n^* = \frac{a_n - ib_n}{2} \frac{a_n + ib_n}{2} = \frac{a_n^2 + b_n^2}{4}\text{.}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Theorem of Dirichlet}

If

\begin{itemize}
    \item $f(x)$ is periodic with period $2L$,
    \item $f(x)$ is single-valued on $[-L,L]$,
    \item $f(x)$ has a finite number of maximum and minimum values on $[-L,L]$,
    \item $f(x)$ has a finite number of discontinuities on $[-L,L]$, and
    \item $\int_{-L}^L |f(x)|~dx < \infty$,
\end{itemize}

then the Fourier series of $f(x)$ converges to $\frac{f(x^+) + f(x^-)}{2}$ for all $x$. That is, at points where $f(x)$ is continuous, the Fourier series simply converges to that value, and at jumps, the series converges to the midpoint of the jump (including at $x = \pm L$).

\hln
\vsp

Some functions that do not satisfy the Dirichlet conditions include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Not single-valued: $x^2 + y^2 = L^2$
    \item Infinite maxima and minima: $f(x) = \sin(1/x)$
    \item Infinite discontinuities: \begin{equation*}
              f(x) = \begin{cases}
                  1  & \text{for } x > 0 \text{ in } \sin(1/x) \\
                  -1 & \text{for } x < 0 \text{ in } \sin(1/x) \\
              \end{cases}
          \end{equation*}
    \item Not absolutely integrable: $f(x) = 1/x$
\end{itemize}

Note that the converse of the theorem of Dirichlet is \emph{not} true. That is, a function that does not satisfy the Dirichlet conditions may still have a convergent Fourier series. In fact, $f(x) = \sin(1/x)$ is such a function.
